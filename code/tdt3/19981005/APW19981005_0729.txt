Conservative Party leader William Hague won overwhelming backing from 
party members for his opposition to a European single currency, and 
said Monday he hoped the party could move away from the damaging splits 
of the past. ``The party has spoken,'' Hague said after winning 84.4 
percent of the vote in favor of his policy of not joining the single 
currency in this Parliament or the next. Effectively, that could rule 
out joining before 2007. ``By any standards, this is a result which 
demonstrates there is a settled will in the Conservative Party to 
fight the next election in favour of keeping the pound and that we 
will certainly do,'' Hague said. The Conservatives, whose 18-year 
run in government ended in a crushing national election defeat last 
year, open their annual conference Tuesday in Bournemouth. Prime Minister 
Tony Blair's government is keeping its options open on the single 
currency, which 11 of the 15 European Union members are launching 
next year. A decision on Britain's membership will almost certainly 
fall to the governing Labor Party, and the Conservative vote was as 
much about intra-party feuding as it is about national policy. More
